# Universal Brainstorming
## Think Before Acting -- Any Topic, Any Platform

Slow down. Understand the idea fully before doing anything.
One conversation spent exploring saves many steps of rework later.

---

## THE CORE PRINCIPLE

> Acting on a half-formed idea produces half-formed results.
> This skill exists to make ideas whole before action begins.

This applies to every domain and every scale:
- A farmer deciding which crop to plant next season
- A developer deciding how to structure a feature
- A writer deciding how to open a chapter
- A founder deciding which market to enter first
- Anyone facing a decision where multiple paths exist

---

## ANTI-PATTERN: "This Is Too Simple To Need Thinking"

Every idea benefits from this process. Small ideas are where unexamined assumptions
cause the most wasted effort. The exploration can be short -- a few exchanges for
truly simple ideas -- but it must happen.

**Do not skip to action without completing at least Steps 1 and 2 below.**

---

## THE 5-STEP FLOW

### Step 1 -- Understand the Context

Before asking anything, understand what already exists:

- What is the current situation? (What is already in place, decided, or constrained?)
- What does the person already know or have?
- What external factors shape this decision? (time, resources, environment, season, market, platform)
- What has already been tried or considered?

Gather this context from the conversation, from any files or documents provided,
or by asking one focused question if the context is unclear.

**Do not proceed to Step 2 until you have a real picture of the starting point.**

---

### Step 2 -- Ask Clarifying Questions (One at a Time)

Ask questions to understand intent, constraints, and success criteria.

Rules:
- One question per message, always
- Prefer multiple-choice over open-ended when options are knowable
- Stop asking once you have enough to propose approaches
- Never ask a question you can reasonably infer from context

Focus areas (adapt to domain -- not all apply to every situation):
- **Purpose:** What outcome does the person want? What problem does this solve?
- **Constraints:** What cannot change? (budget, time, resources, physical limits)
- **Success definition:** How will you know it worked?
- **Audience or context:** Who is this for? Where does it live or operate?
- **Scope:** How big or small should the solution be?
- **Risks:** What could go wrong that matters most?

---

### Step 3 -- Propose 2-3 Approaches

Once you understand the context and intent, propose options -- not one answer.

For each approach:
- Name it clearly (one phrase)
- Describe what it involves in 2-4 sentences
- State what it is best suited for
- State its main trade-off or limitation

Then give your recommendation with a reason.

**Do not present a single answer as if no alternatives exist.**
The person may know something you do not. Options keep the decision theirs.

---

### Step 4 -- Present the Plan

Once an approach is selected (or your recommendation accepted), present a plan.

Scale the plan to the complexity of the task:
- Simple task: 3-5 bullet points covering what, how, and when
- Medium task: structured sections with clear sequence and decision points
- Complex task: phased breakdown with dependencies, risks, and checkpoints noted

Plan dimensions to address (adapt to domain -- not all apply):
- **What:** The specific actions or outputs
- **How:** The method, materials, or approach
- **When:** Sequence, timing, or phasing
- **Risks:** What could go wrong at each step
- **Success check:** How to know each step worked

Present the plan in sections. After each section, ask: "Does this look right so far?"
Do not present the entire plan and wait for one final approval -- validate section by section.

---

### Step 5 -- Hand Off to Action

After the plan is approved:

1. Summarise what was decided in 3-5 sentences (the "what we are doing and why")
2. Confirm the first concrete action to take
3. If any tools, skills, or next steps are relevant -- name them based on what is actually
   available or installed, not from a hardcoded list
4. If a written record would help (for logging, certification, documentation, or reference)
   -- offer to produce one, in whatever format fits the platform and purpose

**The terminal state is: the person knows exactly what to do next and why.**
This skill does not perform the action. It ensures the action is the right one.

---

## DOMAIN ADAPTATION GUIDE

This skill adapts its vocabulary and depth to whatever domain is being explored.
No explicit domain setting is needed -- just follow the context.

| Domain | Context to gather (Step 1) | Plan dimensions that matter most |
|---|---|---|
| Farming / agriculture | Season, soil state, water, inputs available, market | Timing, method, risk of crop failure, cost |
| Business / strategy | Stage, resources, market, competition, constraints | Options, trade-offs, first move, validation step |
| Software / code | Existing stack, scope, users, performance needs | Architecture, sequence, testing, edge cases |
| Writing / content | Audience, purpose, format, tone, length | Structure, key points, opening, what to cut |
| Personal decision | Options available, values, time horizon, reversibility | Trade-offs, what matters most, first step |
| Skill / tool building | Platform, trigger context, what it replaces | Structure, scope, trigger keywords, edge cases |
| Any other domain | Gather: situation, constraints, goal, what exists | Adapt: focus on what shapes the decision most |

---

## KEY PRINCIPLES

- **One question at a time** -- Multiple questions at once stall the conversation
- **Multiple choice preferred** -- Easier to respond to than open-ended when options exist
- **Alternatives, always** -- Propose 2-3 approaches before settling on one
- **Incremental approval** -- Validate each section of the plan before presenting the next
- **Scale to complexity** -- A simple question gets a short plan; a complex one gets a full breakdown
- **Preserve the decision** -- The person decides; this skill informs and structures, not overrides
- **No phantom dependencies** -- Never reference specific tools, skills, or platforms that may not exist
